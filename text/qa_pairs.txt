No. 1
Question
What is the main contribution of your paper in one sentence?

Answer
Our main contribution is a novel method to estimate Shared Mental Models (SMM) continuously and non-intrusively from instant messaging data by combining communication categories and communication dynamics in a graph neural network framework.

No. 4
Question
Your SMM measure is based on perceived SMM. Is that a limitation?

Answer
Yes, we acknowledge that 5-PSMMS measures perceived rather than structural SMM. We chose it because it provides a standardized and scalable measure across real-world teams. We therefore position our work as estimating perceived shared understanding, and we explicitly complement it with agreement measures.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

No. 5
Question
How do you address agreement among team members?

Answer
We explicitly measure within-team agreement using the rwg(j) index, which quantifies consistency across members. We also show a positive correlation between rwg(j) and SMM, suggesting that higher perceived SMM tends to co-occur with stronger agreement.

No. 29
Question
Yeah, so I really like the idea of estimating SMM from communication, I think it's very interesting… but I’m a bit concerned about the fact that you’re using perceived SMM as your ground truth. Because, you know, perceived SMM doesn’t necessarily reflect actual shared cognition, right? And also, since you average across members, I’m wondering how you deal with disagreement within teams… So could you clarify how we should interpret your results given these limitations?

Answer
Thank you, that’s a very important point. You’re absolutely right that our measure is based on perceived SMM rather than structural SMM. We chose 5-PSMMS because it provides a standardized and scalable measure that can be applied across multiple real-world teams, which is essential for our goal of large-scale estimation. To address disagreement, we explicitly compute within-team agreement using rwg(j), which captures how consistent team members’ perceptions are. We also report that agreement correlates with SMM, suggesting that higher SMM scores tend to reflect more aligned perceptions. So our results should be interpreted as estimating perceived shared understanding rather than the exact cognitive structure, and we acknowledge this as an important limitation.

Question

Answer

In that setting, we compared LLM annotations with human annotations and found substantial agreement. We also performed a bootstrap analysis showing high internal consistency. While this does not guarantee perfect correctness, it suggests that the LLM ensemble produces stable and reasonably aligned annotations. We position this as a practical trade-off between privacy and annotation quality.

No. 31
Question
So in your analysis, you selected only a subset of communication categories… and excluded others based on correlation analysis. But I’m wondering… isn’t that a bit ad hoc? Like, how do we know that those excluded categories are actually irrelevant, rather than just noisy in your dataset?

Answer
That’s a great question. Our goal in Study 1 was not to make causal claims, but to identify predictive signals for SMM estimation. We observed that some categories consistently showed low or near-zero correlation with SMM, even after removing sparse cases, suggesting limited predictive contribution. Including those categories actually degraded model performance, which indicates that they function as noise in this context. That said, we do not claim these categories are universally irrelevant—only that they were not useful for prediction in our dataset.

No. 32
Question
I see that you’re using a graph neural network… but I’m wondering whether that’s really necessary. Couldn’t you achieve similar results with simpler models, like just aggregating embeddings or using standard regression?

Answer
Yes, that’s exactly what we tested in our baselines. We compared against models using only text embeddings and models using undirected graphs. The results show that using only embeddings performs poorly, using graph structure improves performance, and combining both communication categories and directed dynamics performs best. This suggests that the relational structure of communication is essential, which is naturally captured by GNNs.

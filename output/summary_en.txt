CALM proposes a fairly radical change to autoregressive language modeling: instead of predicting the next discrete token, it predicts the next continuous vector, where each vector encodes a chunk of K tokens. The motivation is simple but important. Token-by-token generation is a hard sequential bottleneck, and scaling model size alone doesn’t fix it. Their claim is that we need another scaling axis: increase the semantic bandwidth per autoregressive step.

The core mechanism is a lightweight autoencoder. It compresses K tokens into a latent vector and reconstructs them with over 99.9 percent token accuracy. For K equals 4, they first show that reconstruction is possible with very small latents, but for actual language modeling they switch to a more robust 128-dimensional latent. The big challenge is that a plain reconstruction-trained autoencoder produces a brittle latent space: tiny perturbations in z can decode to completely different token sequences. Since a generative model will inevitably make small errors, this breaks downstream modeling.

Their fix is a variational autoencoder-style setup with three stabilizers: KL regularization toward a standard normal, KL clipping to prevent posterior collapse in individual latent dimensions, and dropout both on input tokens and on the latent itself. That combination yields a latent space that is both high-fidelity and robust to noise, which turns out to be essential.

Once text is converted into a sequence of chunk vectors, CALM models those vectors autoregressively with a Transformer backbone plus a lightweight generative head. A key issue is that next-vector prediction has no tractable softmax likelihood, so standard cross-entropy and perplexity are unavailable. Their solution is a likelihood-free framework built around strictly proper scoring rules.

For training, they use an energy-based objective. The generative head samples candidate latent vectors conditioned on the Transformer hidden state, and training minimizes an energy score estimator based on pairwise distances between generated samples and latent targets sampled from the autoencoder posterior. Importantly, they avoid diffusion and flow-matching heads as the default, because those require iterative sampling and would give back much of the efficiency gained from chunking. Instead they use an “Energy Transformer” head that generates a vector in one shot from hidden state plus random noise, using a small residual MLP stack. The head is only about 10 percent of total parameters.

For evaluation, they introduce BrierLM, since perplexity is inapplicable. It adapts the Brier score into a sample-only, likelihood-free LM metric. The estimator uses just two samples from the model and can be extended from tokens to n-grams. Their final metric is the geometric mean of Brier-1 through Brier-4, scaled to 0 to 100. On standard autoregressive models, BrierLM correlates very strongly with cross-entropy, with Pearson minus 0.966 and Spearman minus 0.991, which supports it as a reasonable perplexity surrogate.

They also tackle temperature sampling without explicit probabilities. The exact method is a rejection-sampling algorithm that uses repeated agreement among samples to realize a tempered distribution proportional to P(x) raised to 1 over T. It is theoretically exact for implicit discrete models but can be expensive. So they also give a batch-based approximation that is asymptotically unbiased and much more practical.

Experimentally, the main result is improved performance per unit compute. On WikiText-103, with training on the uncopyrighted Pile, CALM with K equals 4 beats the standard Transformer performance-compute frontier. For example, CALM-M at 371 million parameters gets close to Transformer-S at 281 million, but uses 44 percent fewer training FLOPs and 34 percent fewer inference FLOPs. CALM-L with increasing K shows the central trade-off clearly: K equals 1 is worse than discrete baselines, meaning continuous prediction itself is harder; K equals 2 nearly halves cost with only a small loss; K equals 4 crosses into a better compute-performance regime; K equals 8 degrades, likely because model capacity is insufficient for that bandwidth.

The ablations are informative. The robust latent-space tricks all matter. Diffusion and flow-matching heads work, but the energy head gets the best final quality while keeping single-step generation. For model input, feeding back decoded discrete tokens works much better than feeding latent vectors directly, suggesting the latents are good prediction targets but poor autoregressive inputs.

The broader implication is that next-vector prediction may be a viable path to more efficient language models, especially as context lengths and generation lengths keep growing. The paper’s strongest contribution is not just the chunked representation, but the full supporting toolkit: robust compression, likelihood-free training, evaluation, and sampling. At the same time, the K equals 1 gap versus standard Transformers shows this is still an early-stage paradigm, with clear room for better latent spaces, better generative objectives, and new scaling laws involving semantic bandwidth as a third axis alongside parameters and data.

Behavioural change is often essential for tackling big problems like climate change, but even when the needed actions are clear, people and policymakers often resist them. This research looks at why change spreads slowly and asks whether we can measure, for each person, how much social reinforcement they need before they adopt a new behaviour.

The key idea comes from complex contagion theory. Unlike a simple contagion, where one exposure might be enough, complex contagion assumes that people often need support or confirmation from several others before they change. That point is called an adoption threshold. The challenge is that these thresholds have been important in theory for years, but they have been very hard to measure in practice.

To solve this, the researchers connected complexity science with behavioural science. They reinterpreted adoption thresholds using discrete-choice models, which are commonly used in consumer research to understand how people choose between options. Then they designed two choice-based conjoint experiments. In one, participants repeatedly chose which energy policies to support. In the other, they chose whether to adopt new instant messaging apps. In both studies, the researchers varied how much social support each option appeared to have, so they could observe how social influence affected decisions.

Using those data, they estimated individual adoption thresholds for each participant and each choice context. After that, they ran extensive simulations to test whether these estimated thresholds could improve social change interventions, especially seeding strategies. Seeding means giving an initial push to a small number of people, with the hope that their behaviour will spread through the wider network.

The main result is that interventions work best when they combine two kinds of information: the structure of the social network and the behavioural traits of individuals, especially their adoption thresholds. Approaches based only on network position, or only on behavioural traits, performed worse.

More specifically, when the cost of targeting someone depended mainly on that person’s own preference for the new behaviour, the best strategy was to target people who were connected to many low-threshold individuals. Those nearby low-threshold people are easier to influence and can help spread change faster. But when targeting popular people became more expensive, as in influencer marketing, the best strategy shifted. In that case, the strongest results came from using a measure called complex centrality, which considers both adoption thresholds and the broader pathways through the network, not just immediate contacts.

So the conclusion is that measuring individual thresholds can meaningfully improve interventions for social change. More broadly, the study shows the value of integrating behavioural models, which capture individual decision-making, with complexity models, which explain how changes spread across social systems.

The researchers also note some limitations. Their experiments used relatively simple social cues, which do not capture the full richness of real life, where people respond to different kinds of signals, roles and relationships. Also, in reality, social influence and personal preferences may evolve together over time, and their model does not fully include that.

Even so, the work provides an important proof of concept. It shows that adoption thresholds can be estimated from choice data and then used to design smarter interventions. The next step is to test these ideas in real-world field experiments, with more realistic social settings, to see how well they work outside the lab.

CALM proposes a pretty radical change to autoregressive language modeling: instead of predicting the next discrete token, it predicts the next continuous vector, where each vector encodes a chunk of K tokens. The core motivation is that LLM efficiency is bottlenecked by sequential generation. If one step can carry the information of multiple tokens, you cut the number of autoregressive steps by a factor of K.

The key enabler is a lightweight autoencoder that maps K tokens to a latent vector and back with extremely high fidelity. For K equals 4, they report over 99.9 percent token reconstruction accuracy. But reconstruction alone isn’t enough: a vanilla autoencoder produces a brittle latent space where tiny perturbations decode to unrelated text, which makes downstream generation unstable. So they regularize the latent space with a VAE-style objective, KL clipping to prevent posterior collapse, and dropout both on latent vectors and masked input tokens. That gives them a robust latent manifold where sampled noise around the latent still decodes correctly, again at above 99.9 percent accuracy.

On top of that latent space, CALM trains an autoregressive Transformer over vector sequences rather than token sequences. The hard part is that next-vector prediction is likelihood-free: there’s no finite vocabulary, no softmax, and no tractable density for standard cross-entropy training or perplexity evaluation. Their solution is a full likelihood-free toolkit.

For training, they use an energy-based objective derived from the Energy Score, which is a strictly proper scoring rule. Instead of maximizing likelihood, the model samples candidate vectors and minimizes a sample-based loss that rewards closeness to the true latent while preserving output diversity. Their generative head is an “Energy Transformer”: a small residual MLP stack conditioned on the Transformer hidden state and random noise, designed for single-step vector generation. That choice matters because diffusion or flow-matching heads would reintroduce iterative decoding and undermine the efficiency gains. In their ablations, the energy head beats diffusion and slightly outperforms flow matching, while requiring only one generation step.

One interesting architectural detail is that the model does not feed latent vectors directly back into the Transformer. They tried that, and performance dropped noticeably. Instead, after predicting a latent vector, they decode it back into K discrete tokens, then re-embed and compress those tokens as the next input. So generation is continuous on the output side, but grounded in discrete text on the input side.

For evaluation, they introduce BrierLM, since perplexity is unavailable. It’s based on the Brier score, another strictly proper scoring rule, and can be estimated unbiasedly using only samples from the model. They extend it from single tokens to n-grams, then define BrierLM as the geometric mean of Brier-1 through Brier-4. Empirically, BrierLM tracks cross-entropy extremely well on standard Transformers, with Pearson correlation around negative 0.97 and Spearman around negative 0.99, which is strong evidence that it’s a sensible perplexity substitute for implicit generative models.

They also tackle temperature sampling without access to probabilities. Their exact method uses rejection sampling over repeated draws to simulate sampling from P to the 1 over T distribution. It’s theoretically exact but can be expensive, so they also propose a batch approximation that is asymptotically unbiased and much more practical. Interestingly, in experiments, batch size is a stronger control knob than nominal temperature for trading off diversity and accuracy, and the resulting sampling behavior closely matches standard softmax temperature tuning.

Experimentally, the main result is a better performance-compute trade-off. On WikiText-103, a CALM model with K equals 4 matches or approaches strong Transformer baselines at substantially lower FLOPs. For example, CALM-M, at 371 million parameters, gets BrierLM close to a 281 million parameter Transformer-S while using 44 percent fewer training FLOPs and 34 percent fewer inference FLOPs. Larger CALM models also scale well. Varying K shows the main thesis clearly: K equals 1 underperforms standard discrete modeling, but increasing semantic bandwidth to K equals 2 and especially K equals 4 shifts the frontier favorably. K equals 8 hurts quality more, suggesting current models may lack the capacity to exploit that much compression.

The bigger implication is that CALM introduces semantic bandwidth per generation step as a new scaling axis, alongside parameters and data. That’s the conceptual contribution. It suggests a path toward much more efficient language models, but also raises open questions: better semantically structured autoencoders, more integrated continuous generators, cheaper sampling, and eventually scaling laws that include K as a first-class variable.
